    Siberian Hunters in Their Element

    Werner Herzog’s ‘Happy People: A Year in the Taiga’

    Happy People: A Year in the Taiga

    By NICOLAS RAPOLDJAN. 24, 2013

    Inside

      A consummate explorer-filmmaker, Werner Herzog endeavors in his documentaries to take us into places (and states) we didn’t know we could go to: the Chauvet cave, the Antarctic, the last days of a bear enthusiast. In “Happy People: A Year in the Taiga,” Mr. Herzog airdrops us into the vast Siberian taiga (a sprawling region of subarctic forest) for a seasonal portrait focusing on fur trappers. Rather than his customary foray into the abyss, this film is a quaint throwback to the illustrated chronicles of television nature documentaries or, for that matter, “Nanook of the North.”

      Mr. Herzog created “Happy People” by culling from a TV documentary series by the Russian director Dmitry Vasyukov. (Mr. Vasyukov shares a director’s credit; the film’s editor, Joe Bini, is cited for shaping this “international version.”) Some limitations of adapting secondhand material show through in the uneven visual quality and diminished control over mood. Yet Mr. Herzog is openly inspired, as ever, by the rugged independence of these resourceful trappers, who seem stoic about everything but their faithful dogs.

      He tunes in and out of their lives (with a digression on the struggling Ket people of the taiga) as they wrangle with the wilderness: prepping cabins, setting traps, flushing out furry critters with equally furry dogs. One bearded trapper says he received his stake of land in the early 1970s, suggesting an autonomy that transcends the passing of empires. (Another, Mr. Herzog points out, is related to the filmmaker Andrei Tarkovsky.)

      The trappers are generally treated as actual people — we see an anxious female companion bidding one farewell — rather than as avatars. But Mr. Herzog cannot resist imposing his tendency toward voice-over philosophizing extremity. The documentary stops dead at one point for speechifying about absolute freedom. In a revealing flourish, he explains the film’s title: “Now on their own, the hunters become what they essentially are — happy people.”

      That kind of marveling at courageous, untouched ways of life recalls documentary practices many decades old. As humble as the film as a whole is, the often superfluous narration has a way of highlighting the urge toward showmanship in Mr. Herzog’s work. Sometimes a man surviving winters in the wild with his dog is cool enough.

      A Year in the Taiga

      Opens on Friday in Manhattan.

      Directed by Werner Herzog and Dmitry Vasyukov; written by Mr. Herzog and Rudolph Herzog; narrated by Mr. Herzog; directors of photography, Alexey Matveev, Gleb Stepanov and Arthur Sibirski; edited by Joe Bini; produced by Charlie Woebcken, Christoph Fisser, Vladimir Perepelkin and Nick N. Raslan; released by Music Box Films. At the IFC Center, 323 Avenue of the Americas, at Third Street, Greenwich Village. In English and Russian, with English subtitles. Running time: 1 hour 34 minutes. This film is not rated.
