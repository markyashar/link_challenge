    The Nanny Arrives; Eccentricity Is Squared

    ‘Mental,’ Directed by P. J. Hogan

    Mental

    By STEPHEN HOLDENMARCH 28, 2013

    Inside

      Who is crazy, and who is sane? That question nags at almost all the characters in “Mental,” a hectic, hyperkinetic farce that reunites the Australian director P.J. Hogan with Toni Collette, the star of his hit 1995 comedy, “Muriel’s Wedding.” In “Mental” Ms. Collette plays Shaz, an unkempt, bong-smoking, knife-carrying nanny hired off the street by Barry Moochmore (Anthony LaPaglia), the mayor of Dolphin Heads, a coastal town in Queensland.

      Barry is at his wits’ end when he impulsively invites Shaz, who is hitchhiking with her dog, to watch over his five out-of-control adolescent daughters. A philandering husband and an absent father, Barry has just committed his distraught wife, Shirley (Rebecca Gibney), to a psychiatric hospital and desperately needs someone to look after the children. He is so detached from his family that he can’t even remember his daughters’ names.

      How crazy is Shirley? In the movie’s opening scene, she is shown manically waltzing around trilling “The Sound of Music” while hanging out the family laundry. One of her fantasies has always been to be Maria von Trapp, leading a brood of happy, singing children down an Austrian mountainside.

      But her daughters — Coral (Lily Sullivan), Michelle (Malorie O’Neill), Leanne (Nicole Freeman), Kayleen (Chelsea Bennett) and Jane (Bethany Whitmore) — are undisciplined, self-loathing outcasts who are convinced that they are mentally ill. Coral worries that she is schizophrenic, and Michelle sleeps in a shack outside the house to avoid being spotted by aliens intent on abducting her.

      The neighbors and relatives are just as eccentric. The next-door neighbor Nancy (Kerry Fox) is a neat freak who spends hours every morning scrubbing the driveway with a toothbrush. Shirley’s older sister Doris (Caroline Goodall) makes life-size dolls of children, which she carries around.

      Installed in the Moochmore home, Shaz becomes an assertive surrogate parent who tells the girls that the reason they feel alienated is that they are the next step in the human evolution to perfection. She leads them through exercises in power and subversion, the funniest of which is an invasion of Nancy’s house that involves leaving menstrual blood on her white furniture. To develop self-reliance, Shaz organizes a mountain-climbing expedition.

      When she and the girls disturb the neighborhood calm, “Mental” feels like an extreme satire of 1950s suburban conformism, in which Shaz suggests a female Randle McMurphy (of “One Flew Over the Cuckoo’s Nest”) leading an insurrection. The movie is none too subtle in its portrayal of the “normal” world as even crazier than the mental ward where Shirley is confined.

      “Mental” wildly overplays the kookiness and quirk. An indication that it doesn’t know when to stop is a subplot involving Trevor (Liev Schreiber), a shark hunter who operates an amusement park attraction where Coral works as his assistant. Trevor has a shadowy history with Shaz that includes a daughter who may or may not have been devoured by a shark. With this abrupt and late left turn, the movie loses its tenuous cohesion and turns into a frantic, flailing mess.

      Mental

      Opens on Friday in New York; Los Angeles; San Francisco; Chicago; Phoenix; San Diego; Jacksonville, Fla.; New Haven; Richmond, Va.; Denver; and Seattle.

      Written and directed by P. J. Hogan; director of photography, Donald M. McAlpine; edited by Jill Bilcock; music by Michael Yezerski; production design by Graham Walker; costumes by Tim Chappel; produced by Janet Zucker, Jerry Zucker, Todd Fellman and Jocelyn Moorhouse; released by Dada Films. Running time: 1 hour 56 minutes. This film is not rated.

      WITH: Toni Collette (Shaz), Liev Schreiber (Trevor), Anthony LaPaglia (Barry), Rebecca Gibney (Shirley), Sam Clark (Trout), Kerry Fox (Nancy), Caroline Goodall (Doris), Deborah Mailman (Sandra), Lily Sullivan (Coral), Malorie O’Neill (Michelle), Nicole Freeman (Leanne), Chelsea Bennett (Kayleen) and Bethany Whitmore (Jane).
