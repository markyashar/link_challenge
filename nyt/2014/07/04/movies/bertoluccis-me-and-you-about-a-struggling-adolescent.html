    A Secret Retreat Morphs Into a Detox Unit

    Bertolucci’s ‘Me and You,’ About a Struggling Adolescent

    Me and You

    • NYT Critics’ Pick

    By STEPHEN HOLDENJULY 3, 2014

    Inside

      With his pimply face, mop of tousled hair and blue-eyed stare that conveys a volatile mixture of fear and hostility, Lorenzo, the bratty 14-year-old protagonist of Bernardo Bertolucci’s “Me and You,” is the quintessence of flailing adolescent angst.

      Imagine a neurotic, sullen younger brother of Malcolm McDowell’s troublemaking character Mick Travis in Lindsay Anderson’s 1969 film, “If ...,” and you’ll have a picture of this seething package of pubescent turmoil. If Lorenzo (Jacopo Olmo Antinori) is too consumed in self-absorption to be likable, the performance is a believable portrait of an adolescent who, to use old-fashioned terminology, is “going through a phase.”

      In this small, beautifully made film, which takes place almost entirely indoors, Lorenzo doesn’t crack open his shell and soar so much as break the surface by imploding, then dazedly lurching into the daylight. Instead of going on a ski trip with his classmates, he secretly plans a weeklong staycation, hiding out in a dingy storage space in his family’s apartment building after faking his departure, then sneaking back home. The cellar has a toilet. Its few pieces of shabby furniture are the castoff possessions of an aristocratic woman from whom his father bought the apartment.

      Using the money that his mother, Arianna (Sonia Bergamasco), gives him for expenses, Lorenzo stocks up on junk food and soft drinks. His few belongings include his laptop, an MP3 player with headphones, an Italian translation of “The Vampire Lestat” and an ant farm that he spends hours closely monitoring. The farm, which he cannot successfully manage, is a metaphor for an unruly inner life.

      No sooner has Lorenzo adapted to this claustrophobic world within a world than his privacy is shattered by persistent calls from his mother, whom he convinces that he is enjoying himself on the ski trip, and by the unheralded arrival of his beautiful, mouthy 25-year-old half sister, Olivia (Tea Falco). Initially furious that his solitude has been disturbed, Lorenzo calms down when Olivia vomits on herself and confesses that she has entered the lair to withdraw from heroin, cold turkey. A boyfriend has promised she can join him on a farm in the country, but only if she has kicked drugs.

      The movie doesn’t shy away from depicting the agonies of her withdrawal. We learn, as this edgy relationship develops, that Olivia is a video artist and photographer and see examples of her work on a computer. But her addiction has stymied her creativity.

      “Me and You” is the first film in three decades by this 73-year-old Italian director to be made in Italian, and it is his third consecutive feature, after “Besieged” and “The Dreamers,” whose action takes place in a confined space. Lacking the epic aspirations of “The Last Emperor” or “Little Buddha,” “Me and You,” adapted from a novel by Niccolò Ammaniti, is an exquisite miniature that doesn’t try to tell a story; it’s a meditation on the transformative possibilities of a relationship between troubled half siblings, 11 years apart, who barely know each other. Olivia gives Lorenzo enough of a jolt to awaken him from his trance.

      She hates his beautiful mother, who she believes broke up their father’s marriage. Lorenzo, who never thought of his mother as a home wrecker, has an adolescent love-hate relationship with Arianna and asks her point-blank if she would make love to him to perpetuate the species, were they the last two people on earth.

      Incest is a theme that has long fascinated Mr. Bertolucci, stretching back to “Before the Revolution.” And “Me and You” doesn’t camouflage Lorenzo’s anxious stirrings of desire in the presence of his sexually sophisticated half sister, who dispatches him on missions to get food and sleeping pills to help her withdraw.

      The soundtrack effectively uses songs by the Cure, Arcade Fire and, most prominently, David Bowie to underscore Lorenzo’s state of mind. Mr. Bowie’s hit “Space Oddity,” heard both in Italian and English and the film’s unofficial theme song, evokes the boy’s alienation. He might as well be an astronaut, “floating in my tin can” and cut off from communication to ground control.

      Eventually, of course, he’ll get over it. Growing pains don’t last.

      Me and You

      Opens on Friday in Manhattan.

      Directed by Bernardo Bertolucci; written by Niccolò Ammaniti, Umberto Contarello, Francesca Marciano and Mr. Bertolucci, based on the novel “Io e Te,” by Mr. Ammaniti; director of photography, Fabio Cianchetti; edited by Jacopo Quadri; music by Franco Piersanti; production design by Jean Rabasse; costumes by Metka Kosak; produced by Mario Gianani; released by Emerging Pictures. At Lincoln Plaza, Broadway at 62nd Street. In Italian, with English subtitles. Running time: 1 hour 37 minutes. This film is not rated.

      WITH: Jacopo Olmo Antinori (Lorenzo), Tea Falco (Olivia), Sonia Bergamasco (Arianna), Veronica Lazar (the Grandmother), Tommaso Ragno (Ferdinando) and Pippo Delbono (the Psychologist).
