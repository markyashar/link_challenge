    An Empty Suit, but Such Fine Material

    ‘The French Minister’ Lampoons Power Brokers

    The French Minister

    By MANOHLA DARGISMARCH 20, 2014

    Inside

      Whoosh. When the title character slams a door in Bertrand Tavernier’s dry, sly comedy “The French Minister,” the walls shudder, and papers and people go flying. A man on the furious move, Alexandre Taillard de Worms isn’t only the French minister of foreign affairs, with a name that’s a meaningful mouthful, but he’s also a force of nature or maybe of bureaucracy. Sleek, coifed and buffed, with tailored suits and brilliantly shined shoes, he looks like one of the more sober masters of the universe, even if his violent gestures and the wreckage he leaves in his wake make him a closer kin to that old cartoon menace, the Tasmanian Devil, than to one of the boldface names running La France.

      Embodied chaos, Alexandre is the hub of “The French Minister” and the world he inhabits. Played with sharply clipped gestures and precise comic timing by Thierry Lhermitte, whose torqued physicality here has an almost extracinematic quality — he seems to be sending waves of energy off the screen — Alexandre is a man of many words, some strange, others confusing, many borrowed. When he summons Arthur Vlaminck (Raphaël Personnaz), a graduate of the vaguely Kafka-sounding National School of Administration (a real institution), Alexandre soon seems to be swooping in for the kill. In truth, he’s offering Arthur a job. “Ours is a tiny ship,” Alexander declares — he isn’t one for simply saying: He proclaims, declaims and vows. “Not a ship, even. More a dingy, a canoe.”

      It’s actually more of a raft, Alexandre continues, but there’s room for Arthur, who’s hired to be in charge of “words.” Although Arthur is initially bewildered by the exact parameters of his new position, he has been engaged to write and rewrite Alexandre’s speeches. This proves no easy task, because Alexandre emits large, near-impenetrable clouds of clichés, bulging masses of politician-speak that can build into disquieting Orwellian fogs and that serve as both a weapon and protective shield. It’s hard to make sense of Alexandre, an enigma given to quoting Heraclitus, who had a line that fits the movie nicely: “This world order, the same of all, no god nor man did create, but it ever was and is and will be: ever-living fire, kindling in measures and being quenched in measures.”

      Fires burn, simmer and die, only to rekindle, in “The French Minister,” a comedy of manners and errors. It’s based on “Quai d’Orsay,” a graphic novel that takes its title from the colloquial name for the French Foreign Ministry and its 19th-century wedding cake building. (The rather more blunt title of the book’s English-language version, “Weapons of Mass Diplomacy,” openly telegraphs its comic doings.) Illustrated by Christophe Blain, whose sharp caricatures and comic-book flourishes have been nicely translated to the screen, “Quai d’Orsay” was written by Abel Lanzac, a pseudonym for Antonin Baudry, a diplomat who based it on his experiences working for Dominique de Villepin, the foreign minister during the lead-up to the war in Iraq.

      The names of real people and countries were changed for the book, and Mr. Tavernier, who shares the screenwriting credit with Mr. Baudry and Mr. Blain, has done some additional tweaking. What remains are the general themes, the fighting and squabbling, the gassy proclamations and the slamming doors, as well as the dizzying, unsurprising observation that citadels of power are in the hands of the mad, the craven and the stupid, or maybe just the weary and cynical. Soon after Arthur meets some of his colleagues, including Alexandre’s main fixer, the leonine Claude Maupas (the great Niels Arestrup, commanding his every scene), another worker advises the newbie: “If anybody mentions NATO, say: NATO, No Action Talk Only. Insider speak. Or NATO-rious, for useless. It’s a running gag.”

      Mr. Tavernier’s filmmaking here is loose, almost casual, and you may not always notice what he’s doing with the camera as he frames the ministry’s choreographed chaos with its whirling people and parts. It’s telling, though, that when Arthur first comes to the ministry for his job interview, haplessly dressed down in beige pants and muddied shoes, he’s led through the building’s long, gleaming halls by an aide formally dressed in black tails, like an emissary from a past as disdained as it is fetishized. After he finally reaches the minister’s enormous chambers, Mr. Tavernier pulls the camera back to show Arthur slumped in a chair. Dwarfed by the vaulted ceilings and luxuriously appointed walls, he looks very small in a room and a world that are as empty as they are pompous.

      The French Minister (Quai d’Orsay)

      Opens on Friday in Manhattan.

      Directed by Bertrand Tavernier; written by Antonin Baudry, Christophe Blain and Mr. Tavernier; director of photography, Jérôme Alméras; edited by Guy Lecorne; music by Philippe Sarde; set design by Émile Ghigo; costumes by Caroline de Vivaise; produced by Frédéric Bourboulon and Jérôme Seydoux; released by Sundance Selects. At the IFC Center, 323 Avenue of the Americas, at Third Street, Greenwich Village. In French, with English subtitles. Running time: 1 hour 53 minutes. This film is not rated.

      WITH: Thierry Lhermitte (Alexandre Taillard de Worms), Raphaël Personnaz (Arthur Vlaminck), Niels Arestrup (Claude Maupas), Bruno Raffaelli (Stéphane Cahut), Julie Gayet (Valerie Dumontheil), Anaïs Demoustier (Marina), Thomas Chabrol (Sylvain Marquet), Thierry Frémont (Guillaume Van Effentem), Alix Poisson (Odile), Marie Bunel (Martine), Jean-Marc Roulot (Bertrand Castela), Sonia Rolland (Nathalie), Jane Birkin (Molly Hutchinson) and Didier Bezace (Jean-Paul François).
