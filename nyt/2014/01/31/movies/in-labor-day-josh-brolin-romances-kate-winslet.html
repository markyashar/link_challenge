    Is It Love, or Stockholm Syndrome?

    In ‘Labor Day,’ Josh Brolin Romances Kate Winslet

    Labor Day

    By STEPHEN HOLDENJAN. 30, 2014

    Inside
      Supported by

      Advertisement

      Continue reading the main story
      Continue reading the main story

      The heaving, bubbling euphemism driving Jason Reitman’s “Labor Day” is a juicy homemade peach pie just out of the oven. Its creator, Frank (Josh Brolin), is an escaped prisoner who shows Adele (Kate Winslet), a single mother held captive in her own home, the secrets of baking. That lesson takes place over a sleepy holiday weekend in September 1987. Carefully noting every detail is her nerdy 13-year-old son, Henry (Gattlin Griffith).

      “What I want to talk about is crust,” Frank gravely intones, as the camera, in one of the movie’s many blatant examples of product placement, focuses on a package of Pillsbury flour.

      “It’s all about instinct,” he counsels. “Pay too much attention to recipes, and you forget how to feel.” Then he declares, “People buy all these fancy gadgets, but sometimes the tool is right there attached to your own body.” The fragile, voluptuous Adele begins to quiver perceptibly.

      The hunky Frank, a kind of junior Burt Reynolds, has been serving 20 years for murder when he is seen recovering from an appendectomy at a hospital in the fictitious town of Holton Mills, N.H. Seizing his chance at freedom, he bolts out of a window.

      “Labor Day” occupies the same psychic territory as “Peyton Place,” Grace Metalious’s heavy-breathing 1956 novel, later to be a movie and television series. Both share a similar erotic fantasy of threatening, sexy male dominance. Frank is that fixture of pulp romance, a Real Man who embodies a woman’s secret dreams of a dangerous but sensitive stud as handy in the bedroom and kitchen as he is in the garage.

      At one point, Frank, fully aware of his power, murmurs, “I came to save you, Adele.”

      The story, adapted by Mr. Reitman from Joyce Maynard’s novel, is narrated by the grown-up Henry (Tobey Maguire), an aspiring baker whose future was apparently determined by observing Frank and Adele mooning over pie crust in the kitchen.

      Frank first comes upon Adele and her son in a store where they are picking up supplies. The reclusive, recently divorced Adele is depressed over the breakup of her marriage after a series of miscarriages.

      The fugitive, whose picture is flashed on television, pressures Adele into taking him home, where he can hide out. Once there, he ties her up, then cooks a batch of chili, which he spoon-feeds her like a baby as she gazes up at him beseechingly. The hours pass, and Adele slowly melts, resting her head on Frank’s shoulder while Henry observes, spellbound.

      Cynics might describe “Labor Day” as an instructional manual on S-and-M seduction. Its first lesson is that some light bondage is necessary to show a woman who’s in charge before a man reveals his gentler side. Mr. Brolin speaks softly, but his every word has a grim, authoritarian undertone. He is handsome, with chiseled triceps, but he has a mean face.

      During what becomes a three-day idyll as the besotted lovers hatch a plan to flee to Canada, Frank becomes a surrogate father to Henry, teaching him to play baseball and do other manly things that his real dad, Gerald (Clark Gregg), never bothered to show him. Once Adele surrenders to Frank, the movie pads out the story with incidents in which nosy neighbors and suspicious police officers show up at exactly the wrong moments. Ambiguous flashbacks imply that Frank may not be a monster.

      “Labor Day” is an embarrassing comedown for Mr. Reitman, the gifted director of “Young Adult,” “Juno,” “Thank You for Smoking” and “Up in the Air.” It’s also absurdly prim. The only visible sex in this would-be bodice ripper consists of two less-than-sizzling kisses; the real hanky-panky takes place discreetly and silently behind closed doors. There isn’t a drop of humor.

      The movie’s principal saving grace is Ms. Winslet’s convincing portrayal of Adele, a despairing woman of low self-esteem just a twitch away from a nervous breakdown. In almost every other respect, this overbaked romantic hokum is preposterous.

      “Labor Day” seems designed for the crowd that devours Nicholas Sparks’s romantic daydreams. If it’s a hit, it could generate an uptick in prison correspondence from lonely women to roughnecks behind bars. But how many of those macho outlaws would ever say to a woman what Frank tells Adele as they knead dough: “Pie crust is a very forgiving thing.”

      “Labor Day” is rated PG-13 (Parents strongly cautioned) for brief violence and sexuality.

      Labor Day

      Opens on Friday.

      Written and directed by Jason Reitman, based on the novel by Joyce Maynard; director of photography, Eric Steelberg; edited by Dana E. Glauberman; music by Rolfe Kent; production design by Steve Saklad; costumes by Danny Glicker; produced by Mr. Reitman, Lianne Halfon, Russell Smith and Helen Estabrook; released by Paramount Pictures. Running time: 1 hour 50 minutes.

      WITH: Kate Winslet (Adele), Josh Brolin (Frank), Gattlin Griffith (Henry), Tobey Maguire (Adult Henry), Tom Lipinski (Young Frank), Clark Gregg (Gerald), Brighid Fleming (Eleanor), Maika Monroe (Mandy), Alexie Gilmore (Marjorie), Lucas Hedges (Richard), James Van Der Beek (Officer Treadwell), Brooke Smith (Evelyn), J. K. Simmons (Mr. Jervis), Dylan Minnette (Henry at 16) and Micah Fowler (Barry).
