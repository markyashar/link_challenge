Movie Review | 'Private Property

Private Property (2006)

NYT Critics' PickThis movie has been designated a Critics' Pick by the film reviewers of The New York Times.
New Yorker Films

Isabelle Huppert stars as Pascale in "Private Property."

Forbidden Games: My Two Sons, My Ex-Husband, My Lover and Me

By MANOHLA DARGIS
Published: May 18, 2007
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints

No one bleeds on screen like Isabelle Huppert. I’m talking metaphorically, of course, though given her trembling ferocity, the way this pale sliver of a woman seems to pierce the screen with her absolute commitment to the moment at hand, to the radiance and anguish and bile, it’s hard not to think that she has already sacrificed bits and pieces, gnawing off an arm in one film, opening her veins in another. Watching her in Michael Haneke’s “Piano Teacher” or “Private Property,” a slow-boiling film from the young Belgian Joachim Lafosse, you wonder how much more she can afford to give, how much she has left.

Written by Mr. Lafosse and François Pirot, “Private Property” embraces the banal and the monstrous, and affords Ms. Huppert opportunity to astonish rather than overwhelm. She’s as restrained as Mr. Lafosse’s direction, weeping and still, rather than storming; director and actress twist the knife gently. The filmmaking is as unmannered as the story is uncluttered, with no visual tricks and about a half-dozen speaking parts, including the single mother Pascale (Ms. Huppert) and her teenage twins, Thierry and François, played by real actor-brothers, the blond Jérémie Renier and the darker-haired Yannick Renier. (American filmgoers may recognize Jérémie Renier, having watched him grow up in “La Promesse” and “L’Enfant,” directed by another set of brothers, Luc and Jean-Pierre Dardenne.)

The basics: Pascale and her sons live on the idyllic outskirts of a Belgian town in a rambling, rundown farmhouse. She works at some vague job, while her sons mainly lark about, playing video games and Ping-Pong. She makes the dinner and washes the laundry, and every so often her former husband, Luc (Patrick Descamps), with whom she remains inexplicably at war, drops by with money for the sons. When Pascale scrutinizes her partly bared figure in front of François and a mirror, soliciting the admiration visible in her son’s face — he’s as rapt as a child, charmed as a lover — something doesn’t feel right. And when she showers as Thierry diffidently brushes his teeth next to her, the world seems to stop a beat. Your heart too.

Much like the films of the Dardennes, “Private Property” has the pulse of an emotional thriller, but without commercial cinema’s usual shrieking violins and storytelling beats. The unobtrusive handheld camera keeps close tabs on Pascale and her sons, as if it were another, if rather less-beloved, member of the family. This intimate view can quickly turn claustrophobic, even choking, as in the cannily staged shower scene, which shows Thierry brushing his teeth before a mirror, his naked mother’s image hovering in his sightlines and ours. It has the force of a jolt, this image, partly because it summons up the forbidden, though partly because Mr. Lafosse shoots it without sidelong glances or shadows, letting it simply linger in the ordinary light of day.

Something happens, as it must, as fate comes creeping in. Scenes of casual, almost incidental violence begin to prick the relative calm, as when the sons stomp along a riverbank, forcing rats from their holes in order to shoot them because, as François (the gentler brother) explains, he hates them.

The uneasy hum of this family’s intimacy turns up a notch and then another, prompted by the revelation of Pascale’s affair with a local, Jan (Kris Cuppens). The two adults share their private passion in the back of his car and fumble their buttons in her bed like guilty teenagers. He’s anxious to move away with her, and she seems to want the same, but she’s tethered to her two boys as surely as if she had never cut their cords.

During much of this, Ms. Huppert keeps Pascale’s emotions tamped down, the character’s feelings betrayed only by the blush in the actress’s cheeks. The mother keeps making the meals, keeps ironing the clothes, keeps shuttling her sons back and forth from one destination to another like the howling, hungry infants they increasingly come to resemble. Thierry grows openly scornful, contemptuous, while François clings more forcefully to his mother’s skirts. After a disastrous family dinner with Jan, Pascale takes to her bed, curling up like a fetus, children and parent having traded places, though without the Hollywood-style jokes. Ms. Huppert’s rigorous control in these scenes, her refusal to plead for understanding or forgiveness, is bracing: she puts the role first, not her fear or her narcissism.

In “Private Property,” a film about borders without an ounce of therapy-speak, the ties that bind cinch tighter and tighter. In many respects this is a story about carelessness and about how even the best of intentions, even a mother’s love and a child’s adoration, can go perilously awry. Love itself isn’t enough, Mr. Lafosse seems to say; it’s what you do with that love, whether you use it like a crutch or a smothering blanket, whether you nurture it or let it slip through your fingers. Love makes us cruel. But the beauty of this admirably modest and emotionally true film is that, no matter how profound the cruelty, Mr. Lafosse and his actors never let us forget the love. That might seem terribly sentimental; really, it’s just sentiment.

PRIVATE PROPERTY

Opens today in Manhattan.

Directed by Joachim Lafosse; written (in French, with English subtitles) by Mr. Lafosse and François Pirot; director of photography, Hichame Alaouié; edited by Sophie Vercruysse; produced by Joseph Rouschop and Tarantula (Belgium); released by Red Envelope Entertainment and New Yorker Films. Running time: 95 minutes. This film is not rated.

WITH: Isabelle Huppert (Pascale), Jérémie Renier (Thierry), Yannick Renier (François), Kris Cuppens (Jan), Raphaëlle Lubansu (Anne), Patrick Descamps (Luc), Didier De Neck (Mr. Declerc), Dirk Tuypens (Dirk) and Sabine Riche (Gerda).
